1  Methods

1.1 Pilot 1 (Spring 2022)

Goal: Generate initial items (Version 1) through open-ended surveys and follow-up interviews.

Participants. In Pilot 1, STEM instructors at three institutions— a predominantly White institution (PWI), a Hispanic-serving institution (HSI), and a historically Black college/university (HBCU)—to provide a survey to their current students. A total of 754 students across these institutions completed a survey about a STEM course they were currently enrolled in or had previously taken. Of these participants, 667 were from the PWI, 48 from the HSI, and 38 from the HBCU. Although the sample had relatively few students from the HSI and HBCU, the team closely examined open-ended responses from these smaller groups to ensure coverage of their experiences and conducted follow-up interviews with students from these institutions.

Procedure. Students were randomized to describe either the STEM course that most motivated or least motivated them to pursue the field further. They elaborated on how that course influenced one or more of six psychological experiences: self-efficacy, STEM identity, relevance, connectedness, identity safety, and broad regard. These reports were complemented by semi-structured student interviews and a review of relevant literature.

1.2 Pilot 2 (Fall 2023)

Goal: Test the consistency of student reporting across courses in Version 1, and create Version 2.

Participants. In Pilot 2, 12 instructors across the same institutions had 3,010 unique students participate in the survey. Again, the data was skewed towards the PWI, with 2,298 from the PWI (702 of which were URM), 411 from the HSI, and 35 from the HBCU.

Procedure. Both instructors and students completed surveys approximately four and nine weeks into the semester, typically aligning with the first and second midterm exams. Students completed a questionnaire measuring the presence of the 20 practices in their course and evaluating which practices should be prioritized by the instructor.

A subset of open-ended responses were read through by the research team to capture specific moments in the course when a practice was evident and create a list of themes present in students’ open responses. While we did not calculate formal inter-rater reliability due to the exploratory nature of the coding, multiple researchers independently reviewed responses, and AI-assisted summaries helped elaborate on common themes.

1.3 Pilot 3 (Fall 2024)

Goal: Test whether “Uncertainty” options change participant reports and get some sense of the reporting distributions and predictive validity of practices (albeit, limited to PWI).

Participants. There were 3697 unique students across 49 course/groups with 23 instructors participating, all at a single PWI.

Procedure. Building on insights from Study 2, the research team assembled a broader set of 56 potential practices that were intended to be more precise. Each student was asked about 20 of these practices, and half of the sample was given the choice of selecting an “unsure” option rather than only “yes” or “no.” For data analysis, the “unsure” responses were initially treated as “no” with the assumption that “unsure” responses were more biased more by overall “feelings” in the course rather than objective instructor behaviors occurring. Instructors provided self-reports on the same items, but these data were not the principal focus of this pilot.
